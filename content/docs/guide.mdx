## Introduction

This guide is made for **absolute beginners** who want to learn Japanese in a fun, natural way — by watching anime, reading manga, playing visual novels, and enjoying Japanese content you actually like.

We'll start with the absolute basics, then move into real immersion. Don't worry if you know nothing right now — that's exactly who this is for. Follow the stages in order and you'll have everything you need.

---

## Basics First (Don't Skip This!)

### Hiragana & Katakana

These are the two basic "alphabets" of Japanese — think of them like the ABCs. Every sound in Japanese is written with one of these, so you need to be able to read them before anything else.

The good news: there are only 46 characters in each, and with a bit of daily practice you can get comfortable in 1–2 weeks. Learn Hiragana first, then Katakana.

</Cards>

<Callout type="info">
  Spend 1–2 weeks on this until you can read both without thinking too much. You don't need to be lightning fast — just comfortable.
</Callout>

## Grammar, Vocab & Pitch Accent

### Grammar

Grammar gives you the skeleton of the language — how sentences are built. You don't need to master it or do exercises. Just **read and understand** the basics so things start to make sense when you immerse. You'll internalise it naturally over time.

Pick **one** guide and stick with it:

<Cards>
  <Card title="Tae Kim's Guide" href="https://guidetojapanese.org/learn/" description="Most popular — thorough and free" />
  <Card title="Yokubi" href="https://yoku.bi/" description="Faster and more modern" />
  <Card title="Japanese Ammo with Misa" href="https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLd5-Wp_4tLqYZxS5j3g6kbeOfVXlTkr3N" description="Best if you prefer learning by video" />
</Cards>

### Vocabulary

Use **Anki** — a free flashcard app that shows you words right before you're about to forget them, so you remember more with less effort. It's one of the most powerful tools for language learning.

<Cards>
  <Card title="Download Anki" href="https://apps.ankiweb.net/" description="Free flashcard app for all platforms" />
  <Card title="Kaishi 1.5k Deck" href="https://github.com/donkuri/kaishi" description="The best beginner deck — 1,500 essential words" />
  <Card title="Anki Tutorial" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DcY2Svs3h8M" description="How to set it up and use it properly" />
</Cards>

<Callout type="info">
  <strong>Daily goal:</strong> 5–20 new words + reviews. Consistency matters more than quantity — even 5 words a day adds up fast.
</Callout>

### Kanji

Kanji are the Chinese-origin characters used in Japanese. There are thousands of them, which sounds scary — but don't study them in isolation. **Learn Kanji inside vocabulary**, not as separate symbols. When you learn a word, you're learning its kanji at the same time. Much more natural and efficient.

### Pitch Accent

Pitch accent is the natural rise and fall in the sound of Japanese words — like a melody. It's not essential for beginners, but being aware of it early helps you sound more natural later.

## The Input Stage (Main Stage)

This is where **real learning** happens.

<div className="rounded-3xl border border-violet-500/40 bg-gradient-to-br from-[#2e1065] to-[#1e1b4b] p-8 my-8 shadow-xl">
  <div className="flex items-center gap-3 mb-4">
    <span className="text-3xl">🌊</span>
    <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-violet-300">What is Input / Immersion?</h3>
  </div>
  <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed">
    Immersion means exposing yourself to large amounts of real Japanese — watching, reading, listening — so you build natural intuition for how the language actually works. Rather than memorising rules, you absorb patterns the same way you absorbed your native language growing up.
  </p>
  <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
    This is based on the <strong>Input Hypothesis</strong>: the best way to learn a language is through <strong>comprehensible input</strong> — content you mostly understand, with a little you don't.
  </p>
  <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
    It's like learning to swim — reading about technique helps, but you only really improve when you get in the water.
  </p>
  <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
    <strong>Key Point:</strong> Languages aren't 1:1 translations. Many words have nuances that don't exist in English — particles like は vs が can't be perfectly translated, and words like かける have many different meanings depending on context. Immersion is how you build the natural instinct to understand these without consciously thinking about them.
  </p>
</div>

### The Goal: Automaticity

The aim of immersion is to reach a point where you understand Japanese **automatically** — without having to translate in your head first.

Think about your native language. When someone speaks to you, you don't consciously decode each word — you just understand. That's **automaticity**, and it's what immersion builds over time. Eventually you'll read manga or watch anime and just *get it*, without the mental effort.

<Callout type="info">
  <strong>Important:</strong> Input needs to be <strong>comprehensible</strong> to work. If you understand nothing at all, it's hard to make progress. Aim for content where you get the gist, even if you miss details.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  <strong>What to expect:</strong> You will understand almost nothing at first. It will feel slow and confusing. This is completely normal — progress feels slow, then suddenly accelerates.
</Callout>

## What to Immerse With

You don't have to just watch anime. Any Japanese content you enjoy works — and mixing it up keeps things fresh. Here's a breakdown of the main options and how to get started with each.

### Anime

Anime is the most beginner-friendly option. You get audio, visual context, and subtitles all at once, which makes it much easier to follow along. Use ASBPlayer to load Japanese subtitle files alongside any streaming site.

<Cards>
  <Card title="ASBPlayer" href="https://killergerbah.github.il/asbplayer/" description="Load Japanese subtitle files alongside any streaming video" />
  <Card title="Jimaku.cc" href="https://jimaku.cc/" description="Find Japanese subtitle files for anime" />
  <Card title="Anime Setup Guide" href="https://www.reddit.com/r/LearnJapanese/comments/1iotyp2/use_asbplayer_to_learn_through_anime/" description="Step-by-step ASBPlayer + Yomitan setup" />
</Cards>

Also install **Yomitan** — a browser extension that lets you hover over any Japanese word and instantly see its meaning. Essential for all immersion types.

### Manga

Manga is a great next step after anime — you control the pace, and the visual context helps a lot. To use Yomitan on manga, you need to either run an OCR tool to scan text from images, or pre-process your manga with Mokuro (which makes all the text hoverable directly).

### Visual Novels

Visual novels combine text with voice acting, making them excellent for immersion — you're reading and listening at the same time. You'll need a tool called Textractor to pull the text out of the game so Yomitan can read it.

## How to Immerse (Simple Method)

Think of each sentence like a puzzle. Some pieces (words) you'll recognise, some you won't. Your job is to fit it together — use context to guess, or look things up when needed. Once a sentence clicks, move on to the next one.

<Steps>
  <Step>Open your content of choice with Japanese text/subtitles on</Step>
  <Step>Try to read or follow each sentence as it appears</Step>
  <Step>**0–2 unknown words?** Look them up with Yomitan. **3+ unknown words?** Skip it and keep going.</Step>
  <Step>Spend 30–60 seconds trying to understand, then move on</Step>
</Steps>

**Recommended daily time:** 1–2 hours

### How Often Should You Look Things Up?

There's no single right answer — adjust based on what you're reading or watching:

- **Look up more** when content is hard. This helps you understand deeply and pick up vocab faster, but slows your pace.
- **Look up less** when content is comfortable. This builds faster real-time comprehension, closer to how you'll eventually consume Japanese naturally.

Over time you'll naturally rely on lookups less and less as things become automatic.

### Passive Immersion

You can also immerse during low-effort activities — commuting, cooking, cleaning. Just put on a Japanese podcast or re-watch anime audio in the background.

<Callout type="warn">
  The less focused you are, the fewer gains you'll make. Passive immersion is a useful bonus — not a replacement for active immersion.
</Callout>

### Why Not Just Use Textbooks?

Textbooks teach you *about* the language — the rules, grammar patterns, vocabulary lists. That's useful, but it's not enough on its own. You still need to learn *how* to use those rules in real situations — and that's what immersion does. Immersion is where knowledge turns into instinct.

When you find useful words or sentences while immersing, save them directly into your own Anki deck. Instead of studying random vocabulary, you're studying words from content you've actually consumed — which makes them stick much better.

ASBPlayer (anime), Yomitan (manga/LN), and the VN texthooker setup all support mining with a single shortcut.

## Improving Listening

After a while (mostly applies to anime), start hiding the subtitles and try to listen only. This trains your brain to process spoken Japanese at natural speed — a skill that reading subtitles alone won't build.

<Steps>
  <Step>Hide the subtitles</Step>
  <Step>Listen to a sentence</Step>
  <Step>If you don't understand, turn subtitles back on</Step>
  <Step>Look up 1–2 words if needed</Step>
  <Step>Listen again while reading, then hide subs and try once more</Step>
</Steps>

## Output: Speaking & Writing

Output means producing Japanese yourself — texting, writing, speaking. It's the flip side of immersion, and it's how passive knowledge becomes active.

**Input comes first.** You can't naturally say things you haven't already absorbed. If you find yourself constantly translating from English into Japanese word-by-word, that's a sign you need more input before pushing output hard. A good rule of thumb: if you can read a Japanese sentence and feel when something *sounds off* — even if you can't explain why — you're ready to start.

That said, there's no need to wait until you're "perfect". Chatting casually with people at a low level is fine and won't hurt you.

### What good output actually requires

Output isn't just grammar. There are three things that make Japanese feel natural:

- **Grammar** — getting the structure right (but note: some "wrong" grammar like すごい美味しい has become normal modern usage)
- **Word choice** — using words the way native speakers actually use them, not just translating English phrases directly
- **Social framing** — knowing what to say in a given situation; politeness levels, how to respond to compliments, when to use certain expressions

The third one is the hardest, and it mostly comes from immersing in real conversational content — not textbooks.

### Ways to practice output

- **Chat with native speakers** — language exchange servers (like EJLX Discord), apps like HelloTalk or Tandem, or hire a tutor on italki
- **Write a diary** — write a few sentences in Japanese each day, then re-read them later and mark anything that feels off. You don't need to fix it immediately — just notice it. Your immersion will fill in the gaps over time.
- **Internal narration** — try narrating your day in your head in Japanese. "I'm making tea now." "I wonder what's for dinner." It sounds silly but it surfaces words you don't know yet and gets your brain used to thinking in Japanese.
- **Watch conversational content attentively** — livestreams, podcasts, or casual YouTube videos with two people talking. Pay attention not just to what they say but *how* they say it — transitions, reactions, filler words, how they start and end thoughts.

<Callout type="info">
  The fastest way to improve output isn't drills — it's finding a community or group of people you actually want to talk to in Japanese. When you care about the conversation, the language improves naturally.
</Callout>

## Next Steps After Basics

Once you've been immersing for a few months and things are starting to click, you can start expanding:

- **Light novels** — pure reading, great for pushing your vocabulary and reading speed
- **Visual novels** — story-rich, voiced content; perfect for mixing reading and listening
- **Learn to write** — optional, but useful if you want to communicate in Japanese
- **Try JLPT practice tests** — gives you a clear sense of where you're at
- **Start output** — once you have a solid base of input, see the Output section above
