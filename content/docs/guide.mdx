    ## Introduction
    This guide is made for **absolute beginners** who want to learn Japanese in a fun, natural way — by watching anime, reading manga, playing visual novels, and enjoying Japanese content you actually like.

    We'll start with the absolute basics, then move into real immersion. Don't worry if you know nothing right now — that's exactly who this is for. Follow the stages in order and you'll have everything you need.

    ## Basics First (Don't Skip This!)

    ### Hiragana & Katakana
    These are the two basic "alphabets" of Japanese — think of them like the ABCs. Every sound in Japanese is written with one of these, so you need to be able to read them before anything else.

    The good news: there are only 46 characters in each, and with a bit of daily practice you can get comfortable in 1–2 weeks. Learn Hiragana first, then Katakana.

    <Cards>
      <Card title="RealKana" href="https://realkana.com/" description="Free practice site — learn one row at a time" />
      <Card title="Tofugu Hiragana Guide" href="https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/learn-hiragana/" description="Best visual guide for Hiragana" />
      <Card title="Tofugu Katakana Guide" href="https://www.tofugu.com/japanese/learn-katakana/" description="Best visual guide for Katakana" />
    </Cards>

    <Callout type="info">
      Spend 1–2 weeks on this until you can read both without thinking too much. You don't need to be lightning fast — just comfortable.
    </Callout>

    ## Grammar, Vocab & Pitch Accent

    ### Grammar
    Grammar gives you the skeleton of the language — how sentences are built. You don't need to master it or do exercises. Just **read and understand** the basics so things start to make sense when you immerse. You'll internalise it naturally over time.

    Pick **one** guide and stick with it:

    <Cards>
      <Card title="Tae Kim's Guide" href="https://guidetojapanese.org/learn/" description="Most popular — thorough and free" />
      <Card title="Yokubi" href="https://yoku.bi/" description="Faster and more modern" />
      <Card title="Japanese Ammo with Misa" href="https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLd5-Wp_4tLqYZxS5j3g6kbeOfVXlTkr3N" description="Best if you prefer learning by video" />
    </Cards>

    ### Vocabulary
    Use **Anki** — a free flashcard app that shows you words right before you're about to forget them, so you remember more with less effort. It's one of the most powerful tools for language learning.

    <Cards>
      <Card title="Download Anki" href="https://apps.ankiweb.net/" description="Free flashcard app for all platforms" />
      <Card title="Kaishi 1.5k Deck" href="https://github.com/donkuri/kaishi" description="The best beginner deck — 1,500 essential words" />
      <Card title="Anki Tutorial" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DcY2Svs3h8M" description="How to set it up and use it properly" />
    </Cards>

    <Callout type="info">
      <strong>Daily goal:</strong> 5–20 new words + reviews. Consistency matters more than quantity — even 5 words a day adds up fast.
    </Callout>

    ### Kanji
    Kanji are the Chinese-origin characters used in Japanese. There are thousands of them, which sounds scary — but don't study them in isolation. **Learn Kanji inside vocabulary**, not as separate symbols. When you learn a word, you're learning its kanji at the same time. Much more natural and efficient.

    ### Pitch Accent
    Pitch accent is the natural rise and fall in the sound of Japanese words — like a melody. It's not essential for beginners, but being aware of it early helps you sound more natural later.

    <Cards>
      <Card title="Quick Pitch Accent Guide" href="https://docs.google.com/document/d/1ReBf08JFK4n0PXdOxThAfWuiK9UWVZEWWzeKSECWTQo/edit" description="Short and practical intro to pitch accent" />
      <Card title="Kuuube Minimal Pairs" href="https://kuuuube.github.io/minimal-pairs/" description="Train your ear to hear the difference" />

    ## The Input Stage (Main Stage)

    This is where **real learning** happens.

    <div className="rounded-3xl border border-violet-500/40 bg-gradient-to-br from-[#2e1065] to-[#1e1b4b] p-8 my-8 shadow-xl">
      <div className="flex items-center gap-3 mb-4">
        <span className="text-3xl">🌊</span>
        <h3 className="text-xl font-bold text-violet-300">What is Input / Immersion?</h3>
      </div>
      <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed">
        Immersion means exposing yourself to large amounts of real Japanese — watching, reading, listening — so you build natural intuition for how the language actually works. Rather than memorising rules, you absorb patterns the same way you absorbed your native language growing up.
      </p>
      <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
        This is based on the <strong>Input Hypothesis</strong>: the best way to learn a language is through <strong>comprehensible input</strong> — content you mostly understand, with a little you don't.
      </p>
      <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
        It's like learning to swim — reading about technique helps, but you only really improve when you get in the water.
      </p>
      <p className="text-violet-100 leading-relaxed mt-3">
        <strong>Key Point:</strong> Languages aren't 1:1 translations. Many words have nuances that don't exist in English — particles like は vs が can't be perfectly translated, and words like かける have many different meanings depending on context. Immersion is how you build the natural instinct to understand these without consciously thinking about them.
      </p>
    </div>

    ### The Goal: Automaticity
    The aim of immersion is to reach a point where you understand Japanese **automatically** — without having to translate in your head first.

    Think about your native language. When someone speaks to you, you don't consciously decode each word — you just understand. That's **automaticity**, and it's what immersion builds over time.

    <Callout type="info">
      <strong>Important:</strong> Input needs to be <strong>comprehensible</strong> to work. If you understand nothing at all, it's hard to make progress. Aim for content where you get the gist, even if you miss details.
    </Callout>

    <Callout type="warn">
      <strong>What to expect:</strong> You will understand almost nothing at first. It will feel slow and confusing. This is completely normal — progress feels slow, then suddenly accelerates.
    </Callout>

    ## How to Immerse (Simple Method)

    <Steps>
      <Step>Open your content of choice with Japanese text/subtitles on</Step>
      <Step>Try to read or follow each sentence as it appears</Step>
      <Step>**0–2 unknown words?** Look them up with Yomitan. **3+ unknown words?** Skip it and keep going.</Step>
      <Step>Spend 30–60 seconds trying to understand, then move on</Step>
    </Steps>

    <Cards>
      <Card title="How to Immerse Step-by-Step" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9KlwmHXsXhQ" description="A practical walkthrough of what immersion actually looks like day-to-day" />
    </Cards>

    <Callout type="info">
      Start with content you already know — a manga you've read or an anime you've seen in English.
    </Callout>

    **Recommended daily time:** 1–2 hours

    ### Passive Immersion
    <Callout type="warn">
      The less focused you are, the fewer gains you'll make. Passive immersion is a useful bonus — not a replacement for active immersion.
    </Callout>

    ## Output: Speaking & Writing

    **Input comes first.** You can't naturally say things you haven't already absorbed.

    ### What good output actually requires
    - **Grammar** — getting the structure right
    - **Word choice** — using words the way native speakers actually use them
    - **Social framing** — knowing what to say in a given situation

    ### Ways to practice output
    - Chat with native speakers (HelloTalk, Tandem, italki, EJLX Discord)
    - Write a diary
    - Internal narration
    - Watch conversational content attentively

    <Callout type="info">
      The fastest way to improve output is finding a community or group of people you actually want to talk to in Japanese.
    </Callout>
