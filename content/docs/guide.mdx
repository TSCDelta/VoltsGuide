#### Grammar

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Grammar gives you the skeleton of the language — how sentences are built. You don't need to master it or do exercises. Just <strong>read and understand</strong> the basics so things start to make sense when you immerse. You'll internalise it naturally over time.</p>
  <p>Pick <strong>one</strong> guide and stick with it:</p>
</div>

#### Vocabulary

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Use <strong>Anki</strong> — a free flashcard app that lets you learn words and review them right before you forget them, so you remember more with less effort. It's one of the most powerful tools for language learning.</p>
</div>

<Cards>
  <Card title="Download Anki" href="https://apps.ankiweb.net/" description="Free flashcard app for all platforms" />

<Callout type="info">
  **Daily goal:** 5–20 new words + reviews. Consistency matters more than quantity — even 5 words a day adds up fast.
</Callout>

<span id="kanji" className="guide-anchor" />

#### Kanji

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Kanji are characters used in Japanese to represent words and meanings. There are thousands of them, which may sound overwhelming — but don’t worry, you won’t learn them all at once or study them in isolation. <strong>Learn Kanji inside vocabulary</strong>, not as separate symbols. When you learn a word, you're learning its kanji at the same time. Much more natural and efficient. This [video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=exkXaVYvb68) explains how.</p>
</div>

<span id="pitch" className="guide-anchor" />

#### Pitch Accent

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Pitch accent is the way certain parts of a Japanese word are spoken higher or lower in pitch. You do not need to focus on it as a beginner, but being aware of it early can help you sound more natural over time.</p>
</div>

<span id="what-is-immersion" className="guide-anchor" />

<div className="guide-infobox">
  <div className="guide-infobox-header">
    <span className="guide-infobox-emoji">🌊</span>
    <p className="guide-infobox-title">What is Input / Immersion?</p>
  </div>
  <p>Immersion means exposing yourself to large amounts of real Japanese — watching, reading, listening — so you build natural intuition for how the language actually works. Rather than memorising rules, you absorb patterns the same way you absorbed your native language growing up.</p>
  <p>This is based on the <strong>Input Hypothesis</strong>: the best way to learn a language is through <strong>comprehensible input</strong> — content you mostly understand, with a little you don't.</p>
  <p>It's like learning to swim — reading about technique helps, but you only really improve when you get in the water.</p>
  <p><strong>Key Point:</strong> Languages aren't 1:1 translations. Many words have nuances that don't exist in English — particles like は vs が can't be perfectly translated, and words like かける have many different meanings depending on context. Immersion is how you build the natural instinct to understand these without consciously thinking about them.</p>
</div>

#### The Goal: Automaticity

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>The aim of immersion is to reach a point where you understand Japanese <strong>automatically</strong> — without having to translate in your head first.</p>
  <p>Think about your native language. When someone speaks to you, you don't consciously decode each word — you just understand. That's <strong>automaticity</strong>, and it's what immersion builds over time. Eventually you'll read manga or watch anime and just <em>get it</em>, without the mental effort.</p>
</div>

<Callout type="info">
  **Important:** Input needs to be **comprehensible** to work. If you understand nothing at all, it's hard to make progress. Aim for content where you get the gist, even if you miss details.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  **What to expect:** You will understand almost nothing at first. It will feel slow and confusing. This is completely normal — progress feels slow, then suddenly accelerates.
</Callout>

## How to Immerse

<p className="guide-intro">Think of each sentence like a puzzle. Some pieces (words) you'll recognise, some you won't. Your job is to fit it together — use context to guess, or look things up when needed. Once a sentence clicks, move on to the next one.</p>

<div className="guide-card">
  <ol className="guide-steps-list">
    <li>Open your content of choice with Japanese text/subtitles on</li>
    <li>Try to read or follow each sentence as it appears</li>
    <li><strong>0–2 unknown words?</strong> Look them up with Yomitan. <strong>3+ unknown words?</strong> Skip it and keep going.</li>
    <li>Spend 30–60 seconds trying to understand, then move on</li>
  </ol>
</div>

<Callout type="info">
  Start with content you already know — a manga you've read or an anime you've seen in English. Knowing the story gives you a huge boost in understanding.
</Callout>

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>There's no single right answer on how often to look things up — adjust based on what you're reading or watching:</p>
  <ul className="guide-bullet-list">
    <li><strong>Look up more</strong> when content is hard. This helps you understand deeply and pick up vocab faster, but slows your pace.</li>
    <li><strong>Look up less</strong> when content is comfortable. This builds faster real-time comprehension, closer to how you'll eventually consume Japanese naturally.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Over time you'll naturally rely on lookups less and less as things become automatic.</p>
</div>

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>You can immerse with anime, manga, visual novels, light novels, or anything you enjoy. Each has different setup requirements — see the <a href="/docs/resources">Resources page</a> for tool guides and recommendations for each content type.</p>
</div>

<div className="guide-routine guide-routine--green">
  <div className="guide-routine-header">
    <span>🎯</span>
    <p className="guide-routine-title">Daily Immersion Routine</p>
  </div>
  <ul className="guide-routine-list">
    <li>1–2 hours of your chosen content (anime, manga, VN, LN)</li>
    <li>Look up 0–2 unknown words per sentence</li>
    <li>Sentence mine good words into Anki</li>
    <li>For anime: later hide subs and focus on listening</li>
    <li>Optional: passive immersion during low-focus tasks</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## Sentence Mining 

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>When you find useful words or sentences while immersing, save them directly into your own Anki deck. Instead of studying random vocabulary, you're studying words from content you've actually consumed — which makes them stick much better.</p>
  <p>ASBPlayer (anime), Yomitan (manga/LN), and the VN texthooker setup all support mining with a single shortcut. This video is a good [intro](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B60cj69MSmA) to sentence mining. See the <a href="/docs/resources">Resources page</a> for setup guides.</p>
</div>

<span id="speaking" className="guide-anchor" />

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Output means producing Japanese yourself — texting, writing, speaking. It's the flip side of immersion, and it's how passive knowledge becomes active.</p>
  <p><strong>Input comes first.</strong> You can't naturally say things you haven't already absorbed. If you find yourself constantly translating from English into Japanese word-by-word, that's a sign you need more input before pushing output hard. A good rule of thumb: if you can read a Japanese sentence and feel when something <em>sounds off</em> — even if you can't explain why — you're ready to start.</p>
  <p>That said, there's no need to wait until you're "perfect". Chatting casually with people at a low level is fine and won't hurt you.</p>
</div>

### What good output actually requires

<div className="guide-card">
  <p>Output isn't just grammar. There are three things that make Japanese feel natural:</p>
  <ul className="guide-bullet-list">
    <li><strong>Grammar</strong> — getting the structure right (but note: some "wrong" grammar like すごい美味しい has become normal modern usage)</li>
    <li><strong>Word choice</strong> — using words the way native speakers actually use them, not just translating English phrases directly</li>
    <li><strong>Social framing</strong> — knowing what to say in a given situation; politeness levels, how to respond to compliments, when to use certain expressions</li>
  </ul>
  <p>The third one is the hardest, and it mostly comes from immersing in real conversational content — not textbooks.</p>
</div>

### Practicing Writing

<div className="guide-card">
  <p> Practicing writing is really easy. For kana, you can use [this](https://ankiweb.net/shared/info/1146619527) to learn to write. For kanji, you can use [this](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1lFvmBAVPLB_t-g3l-jR1BVWFIiZbEgdO) to learn how to write. Alternatively, you can use <strong>RTK</strong> to learn how to write kanji if you prefer textbooks.</p>
</div>

### Ways to practice speaking

<div className="guide-card">
  <ul className="guide-bullet-list">
    <li><strong>Chat with native speakers</strong> — language exchange servers (like EJLX Discord), apps like HelloTalk or Tandem, or hire a tutor on italki</li>
    <li><strong>Write a diary</strong> — write a few sentences in Japanese each day, then re-read them later and mark anything that feels off. You don't need to fix it immediately — just notice it. Your immersion will fill in the gaps over time.</li>
    <li><strong>Internal narration</strong> — try narrating your day in your head in Japanese. "I'm making tea now." It sounds silly but it surfaces words you don't know yet and gets your brain used to thinking in Japanese.</li>
    <li><strong>Watch conversational content attentively</strong> — livestreams, podcasts, or casual YouTube videos with two people talking. Pay attention not just to what they say but how they say it — transitions, reactions, filler words, how they start and end thoughts.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Callout type="info">
  The fastest way to improve output isn't drills — it's finding a community or group of people you actually want to talk to in Japanese. When you care about the conversation, the language improves naturally.
</Callout>
